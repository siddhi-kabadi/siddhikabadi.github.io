Introduction

Text passwords remain the dominant authentication mechanism, yet public understanding of what makes a password strong remains fragmented. Existing approaches rely on the LUDS mechanism — lowercase, uppercase, digits, and symbols — but prior studies conclude these quantitative heuristics are often ineffective, overlooking linguistic patterns like l33tspeak.

Entropy-based metrics like zxcvbn offer improvements, but a gap remains between rule-based and learned representations. This project bridges that gap with a fine-tuned BERT classifier trained on nearly half a million real passwords.

"Can a language model learn what makes a password strong — beyond the rules humans write?"
02

Objectives

  • 01Analyze structural and linguistic patterns contributing to password bias and vulnerability
  • 02Fine-tune a BERT-based classifier to learn contextual dependencies beyond rule-based heuristics
  • 03Evaluate relationships between password features (LUDS) and strength classifications
  • 04Apply graphical modeling and KMeans clustering to identify structural patterns
  • 05Compare learned model outputs with zxcvbn, the leading entropy-based estimator
03

Methodology

The labeled Kaggle dataset (468,747 entries) was split 70/30 for training and testing. BERT (bert-base-cased) was chosen specifically for its sensitivity to letter casing — a key feature in password security — and fine-tuned with a linear classifier over 3 epochs using AdamW optimization and cross-entropy loss.

Post-training, the model was applied to two unlabeled datasets: 10,000 common passwords and 10,000 randomly generated passwords. Outputs were analyzed through correlation heatmaps, bar charts, and KMeans clustering across four features: password length, symbol count, digit count, and uppercase letter usage.

KMeans Clustering

Weak passwords formed a homogeneous cluster with minimal feature diversity. Strong passwords exhibited far greater variability — confirming that strength correlates with structural unpredictability.

BERT vs. zxcvbn

The two systems agreed on 2,871 weak and 4,070 strong passwords. Key divergence emerged at medium strength, where zxcvbn uses pattern-matching rules while BERT evaluates learned linguistic context.

06

Conclusion

Results show a consistent correlation between password length, uppercase characters, symbols, digits, and overall strength — features also codified in LUDS. This suggests that BERT's classifications align with established theory, and that zxcvbn continues to draw on similar structural principles.

Fine-tuned language models can serve as powerful, complementary tools in password strength evaluation — particularly for capturing contextual and linguistic patterns that rule-based systems miss.
